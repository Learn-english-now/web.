Vowels and Consonants

Learn the building blocks of words: vowels and consonants.

Introduction

In English, letters are divided into two categories: vowels and consonants. These groups form the foundation of all words and sounds in the language.

What Are Vowels?

Vowels are the sounds produced without blocking airflow from the mouth. There are five main vowel letters in English: A, E, I, O, U, and sometimes Y can act as a vowel.

Example: apple (a), elephant (e), igloo (i), octopus (o), umbrella (u).

What Are Consonants?

Consonants are the letters that are pronounced with some obstruction of the airflow by the lips, teeth, or tongue. There are 21 consonants in the English alphabet.

Example: b, c, d, f, g, h, j, k, l, m, n, p, q, r, s, t, v, w, x, y, z.

Differences Between Vowels and Consonants

A, E, I, O, U (and sometimes Y)All other letters of the alphabet

Why Are They Important?

Understanding vowels and consonants is key to learning pronunciation, spelling, and phonics. Every syllable in English contains at least one vowel sound, making them essential to word formation.
