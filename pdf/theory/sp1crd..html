British vs. American Spelling

A complete guide to the spelling differences in English varieties.

Introduction

British and American English share the same roots, but their spelling systems have evolved differently over time. These differences are mainly due to historical reforms, cultural influences, and attempts to simplify the language. Noah Webster, an American lexicographer, played a major role in modifying and simplifying English spelling for American usage.

Why Are There Differences?

In the 18th and 19th centuries, Webster believed that English spelling was overly complicated and inconsistent. His dictionary introduced changes to make spelling closer to pronunciation. Meanwhile, British English preserved many of its traditional spellings that were inherited from French and Latin influences.

Example: British English keeps the "u" in "colour", while American English simplifies it to "color".

Main Categories of Differences

1. Words Ending in –our vs. –or

American English drops the "u" in words like colour → color, favour → favor, honour → honor.

ColourColor

2. Words Ending in –re vs. –er

British English often uses –re, while American English flips it to –er.

CentreCenter

3. Words Ending in –ise vs. –ize

British English prefers –ise, while American English uses –ize (organise → organize).

4. Double Consonants

British English doubles consonants in certain cases (travelling, labelled), while American English simplifies them (traveling, labeled).

Examples of Other Differences

  • British: cheque | American: check
  • British: jewellery | American: jewelry
  • British: tyre | American: tire
  • British: programme | American: program

Which Spelling Should You Use?

The choice of spelling depends on your target audience. Academic and professional settings require consistency, so you should stick to either British or American conventions throughout your writing.
